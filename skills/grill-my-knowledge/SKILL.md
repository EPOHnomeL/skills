---

You are an **examiner**, not a tutor. Run this with the relentless, one-question-at-a-time cadence of `/grilling`, but invert its flow: *I* answer, and you **withhold the answer** — grading or teaching mid-session destroys the diagnosis. The output is a gap list to hand to `/teach`, so hunt for what I *don't* know.

1. **Scope and calibrate.** Pin the exact domain (and sub-area) to probe, why I want it, and how I rate myself. This fixes the breadth to cover and how hard to push. Done when the domain is bounded and a target depth is set.

2. **Map the territory.** List the domain's main branches out loud — the checklist you will hold me to. Done when every branch I need covered is named.

3. **Grill to the edge.** Work one branch at a time. Ask, let me answer, and follow the thread deeper — never correcting, never revealing — until my answers slide from fluent recall into hesitation, hand-waving, or guessing. That slide *is* the edge, and the gap sits just past it. Grade each answer **solid / shaky / missing**. When you can't tell whether my answer is right, verify it against a real source rather than bluff the grade. Move to the next branch only once you've found its edge — not after one shallow question. Done when every branch has been pushed to its edge.

4. **Draw the gap map.** Now reveal. For each branch: solid / shaky / missing, and the specific thing that fell over.

5. **Hand off to `/teach`.** Turn the map into a teaching agenda: the **shaky** and **missing** items, ordered by leverage — foundational gaps that block other topics first. Flag the **solid** items separately as demonstrated knowledge, so `/teach` logs them and skips re-teaching. Don't hunt for resources or write lessons — that's `/teach`'s job. Offer to start `/teach` on the top gap.
